Reflecting on Lectures So Far
13 November 2017
So far we have had three guest lectures from people within the industry. Below I will write a little bit about each one and what impact they left on me!


Oskar Burman from Fast Travel Games
It was very interesting to hear from Oskar Burman about the potential of VR gaming in the future. He seems to be very optimistic about the potential market for said games and that it will only get better. Some of the key parts I took away from the presentation was how to divide up games into categories when looking at platforms. Although they were so obvious to me - hearing it listed out with graphics made me think. A phone game is usually played in short bursts but multiple times throughout the day. So the goal of a phone game developer is to create a game that the user can not only enjoy for a couple of minutes, but a game that will also bring them back at a later time. This is most likely why the cooldown style games are so popular these days. By this I mean games that have for example, after you perform one important thing in the game, you cannot perform the same action again until 8 hours later UNLESS you buy this sweet power up. This is how the game company earns money from the free to play genre. The goal is to find a fine balance where you give the user the option to pay or wait with end goal of bringing them back to play.

The second category dealt with traditional games. For most people you know that when you sit down and play you generally play for a couple of hours or as long as you can. This works because real console and computer games are designed to be played that way to begin with - you expect the user to play for a longer period of time. VR now becomes the third category and this is where things get interesting. A VR requires, essentially, an appointment. There is a lot of setup required and generally a player does not play for too long as it is quiet an exhaustive experience. Oskar Burman said that a VR session is approximately 30 minutes at a time. This may however mean that after the session is over, the user takes a bit of a break and then jumps back into it again after they've recovered. So designing a game that allows for this type of play is an interesting process!


Jörgen Björklund from Paradox
I really enjoyed this lecture as we got to hear insights on how the publishing world works - perhaps something normal gamers don't think about when accusing developers for the 'misdoings' of a game. There is huge heated discussion online about monetization models, specifically in game lootboxes, and it was interesting to hear from Jörgen how most of the decisions are made as this shed some light on the situations happening in the real world (I am of course right now talking about Starwars Battlefront 2 by DICE and the huge controversy surrounding this). I did not know for example that once a publishing company agrees to a project (after much 'input' from them as to how the game should be) they OWN the intellectual property. Of course it makes sense to a certain extent as they are the capital venturists and want to have exhaustive control over the project they funded. However from a creator's perspective it must feel very weird to sell your idea just like that. He did discuss what happens when a publisher and developer fall through and that it does happens ocassionally but it's very scary to think about.


Tommy Palm from Resolution Games
The presentation by Tommy Palm gave a cool insight into how mobile games are designed to be profitable and a brief history on what they were prior to the iPhone. It was very interesting to hear him talk about the future of VR and mobile gaming as well.
