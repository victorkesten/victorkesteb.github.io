This post will be a bit longer as it is a reflection on all the presentations given today, as well as a thorough final thoughts moment on our project!

Kamiclones

I really enjoyed hearing about this game! I think it's cool that they created their own Java engine and including a bunch of different libraries beyond Unity. I know that Daniel, their main programmer, is very comfortable using Java as his main programming language and has used this to create previous games. So it's great to see him incorporate this knowledge into this course! The gameplay has a lot of potential and I could totally see this being a hit. I like ragdoll physics and the way to use 'clones' to create a path in order for you to beat the level is very neat.

Arr

I personally am not invested in this type of genre but I believe the end result of their game was very cool compared to what they previously showed. I know that working with AR based technology can be very difficult and therefore it's cool to see that they created a very functional and neat game based on just Vuforia and marker tracking. Like some of the questions/comments after their presentation said - it would be very cool for them to incorporate ARKit/ARCore and have markerless surfaces to really create a different sense of immersion. However, a very good and valiant effort on there part. I was impressed!

BAM

This game was a bit out of my league. I have never played Football Manager but I know of the game type. It seems that this game is very mechanic and strategy heavy and I honestly believe they failed to show this importance during the presentation. I also gave them a suggestion to show which team is which once in game, but this was appearntly an afterthought and something they didn't have time to implement. Perhaps not a game I would buy, but surely there is a niche market for it.

Puzzle Tower VR

Wow! This game was very cool to hear about and I love how they tested it on a real target group. Their primary target audience was on younger kids and they successfully tested it on said audience. I think the game itself is very cool and I could see it being expanded into something fullfledge and successful given enough time. I also really enjoyed hearing their business plan as it was EXTREMLEY detailed and well thought throught! Well done to this team!

Defense of Saturn

I saw much potential in this game yet I believe they failed to deliver. One of my major criticism was the camera angle and how I sort of lost the 'depth' feeling. This could've potentially been solved by allowing the main camera to move around, sicne they were exploring 3D graphics anyways. Another slight disappointment is that they promised a lot during the early demos and status updates that sounded very exciting yet failed to deliver on them for the final demo. What was good however was the modelling and the animations for shooting. I also liked that they used the Overwatch font (because I personally think it's an awesome font). However, it failed the 'turret defense' feeling by not having enemy collision when attempting to build a maze. Surely given more time, they could've made this game great but I believe it sort of failed to deliver for this demo in terms of how they hyped it during prior presentations.

Quest

This 'game' did not really feel like a game to me. Perhaps this project would have been more suited for a course such as Advanced Graphics and Interaction but for a "Game Design" course it felt a bit... lost. Sure - they had a 'quiz' element to location baesd points, but where is the game component? A quiz with multiple choise as basic as it gets for a game. You can explore AR and gaming in so many different ways, such as Arr. Although a very interesting project if continued, it really felt misplaced for the course given almost 0 'game' elements. But perhaps I am just being a bit too harsh.

Once Upon A Time

This was one of my favorites! I love the artwork for the game, I love the level design and I love the overall themes and stories that are told. I think what made it so great is that they early on in the presentation put up a link where we can download and test it ourselves during the presentation and thus giving us a better understanding of what they were talking about! Tihs is soemthing a few other groups perhaps 'failed' at - i.e providing a demo for everyone to try. Sure, only one level was available, but as a demo this was perfect. The level featured great quality and they showed the potential expansions of the future. I thoroughly enjoyed their presentation!

Knock Off

Although the game design for this game is relatively simple I really loved the way they presented the game. This is kind of what games have to do to make me notice them - blow me away with some crazy aspect. For this it was the characters - I love that it was world leaders and that they stuck to this theme. It made it all so much fun! And yes - I do believe they stayed true to their "Quake" themed aspect. I also felt elements of Super Smash Bros and other brawling games. Also once again - having a live demo that people could test made it even more fun and I believe everyone truly enjoyed the presentation they put on.

Andreas Linn - DICE
4 December 2017


Today's lecture was held by Andreas Linn from DICE. He is a gameplay programmer - a work description that seemed fairly vague at first but I believe he did a good job explaining what he does. A gameplay programmer, at dice, essentially handles bug detection and feature additions when it comes to direct gameplay. If a designer asks for something to be available for a new weapon they design, the gameplay programmer essentially creates the functionality of this function within the game engine. He also gave an example that if for an example, a parachute wasn't working the way they intend it to, he is the one who fixes said bug. I believe this presentation was one of the better ones as he gave a very clear description of his job, to the best of his ability. It wasn't a pitch about why VR/AR is the future, but it really felt like a back-to-basic, "hey i am a programmer at dice. This is what we do and how coding pipelines work" which I am ver interested in. I also enjoyed listening to the solutions to some of the technical challenges that occur when working with larger projects, such as the Git solution and how multiple people work on the same scene.

Reflecting on Lectures So Far
13 November 2017
So far we have had three guest lectures from people within the industry. Below I will write a little bit about each one and what impact they left on me!


Oskar Burman from Fast Travel Games
It was very interesting to hear from Oskar Burman about the potential of VR gaming in the future. He seems to be very optimistic about the potential market for said games and that it will only get better. Some of the key parts I took away from the presentation was how to divide up games into categories when looking at platforms. Although they were so obvious to me - hearing it listed out with graphics made me think. A phone game is usually played in short bursts but multiple times throughout the day. So the goal of a phone game developer is to create a game that the user can not only enjoy for a couple of minutes, but a game that will also bring them back at a later time. This is most likely why the cooldown style games are so popular these days. By this I mean games that have for example, after you perform one important thing in the game, you cannot perform the same action again until 8 hours later UNLESS you buy this sweet power up. This is how the game company earns money from the free to play genre. The goal is to find a fine balance where you give the user the option to pay or wait with end goal of bringing them back to play.

The second category dealt with traditional games. For most people you know that when you sit down and play you generally play for a couple of hours or as long as you can. This works because real console and computer games are designed to be played that way to begin with - you expect the user to play for a longer period of time. VR now becomes the third category and this is where things get interesting. A VR requires, essentially, an appointment. There is a lot of setup required and generally a player does not play for too long as it is quiet an exhaustive experience. Oskar Burman said that a VR session is approximately 30 minutes at a time. This may however mean that after the session is over, the user takes a bit of a break and then jumps back into it again after they've recovered. So designing a game that allows for this type of play is an interesting process!


Jörgen Björklund from Paradox
I really enjoyed this lecture as we got to hear insights on how the publishing world works - perhaps something normal gamers don't think about when accusing developers for the 'misdoings' of a game. There is huge heated discussion online about monetization models, specifically in game lootboxes, and it was interesting to hear from Jörgen how most of the decisions are made as this shed some light on the situations happening in the real world (I am of course right now talking about Starwars Battlefront 2 by DICE and the huge controversy surrounding this). I did not know for example that once a publishing company agrees to a project (after much 'input' from them as to how the game should be) they OWN the intellectual property. Of course it makes sense to a certain extent as they are the capital venturists and want to have exhaustive control over the project they funded. However from a creator's perspective it must feel very weird to sell your idea just like that. He did discuss what happens when a publisher and developer fall through and that it does happens ocassionally but it's very scary to think about.


Tommy Palm from Resolution Games
The presentation by Tommy Palm gave a cool insight into how mobile games are designed to be profitable and a brief history on what they were prior to the iPhone. It was very interesting to hear him talk about the future of VR and mobile gaming as well.
